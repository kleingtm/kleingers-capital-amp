What is Cryptocurrency?

Cryptocurrency is simply digital money.

It also happens to be based on blockchain technology - the ins and outs of which will be covered in other blog posts.

The short version?

Cryptocurrency allows any person on this earth to send money to any other person. Without a bank. Rather than relying on the trust of a company, lawyer, or piece of legislation to honor an account or transaction, cryptocurrency is “trust-less”. The need for trust is simply replaced by math and computer science.

It’s incredible if you think about it.

Think of all the money invested in the current financial industry - from banking salaries, to lawyers, to e-commerce websites charging ~2.9% per transaction (modern cryptocurrencies offer ~.05 cents flat, or better), to the brick and mortar infrastructure of financial institutions.

All of it - potentially replaced by a smarter, way more efficient tool.

No doubt, we’re in the earliest stage of cryptocurrency. It may take five years. It may take ten or more. But the existing financial inefficiencies are losing ground. And, as proven time and time again throughout history, the superior technology eventually wins.

So what is cryptocurrency?

  • Cryptocurrency is digital money.
  • It does not require a bank.
  • It cannot be inflated.
  • It is not subject to a government or central power changing the rules halfway through the game.
  • It democratizes money by limiting the control of the privileged few (banks) over the many.

A global exchange of wealth - the likes of which the world has never seen - is in the works. And as you can imagine, the privileged few won’t be giving up control without a fight.

Dealing with Critics

At some point, you’ve sat around the dinner table, coffee bar, water cooler, etc, and heard from the cryptocurrency critics.

Rather than go through this post with predefined bias, why not confront the two most commonly repeated sound bites upfront?

Cryptocurrency Has No Inherent Value

The number one criticism. And you know what? I agree. Cryptocurrency has zero inherent value.

And neither does the US dollar, which drives the world economy.

The Need for Currency

Currency was quite literally designed  for the purpose of abstracting away inherent value.

Why? Because it’s extremely inefficient to carry around ounces of gold, barrels of oil, horses, or other items people may or may not value.

It’s not like the world hasn’t tried it. Many economic models throughout history consisted of bartering for inherently valued goods. And all of those economies were extremely inefficient.

Say, for example, you were a blacksmith and needed a horse. Unfortunately, the person selling a horse doesn’t need anything made of metal and happens to value gold. You then began tracking down the person holding gold. As it turns out, the person holding gold only values food for feeding a family. You see where this is going…

If someone wants to make the “inherent value” argument, that’s certainly their prerogative.

But the underlying notion is that we should be blacksmiths bartering for horses.

Currency Should Be Backed by Something of Inherent Value

“Of course currencies are necessary. But they have to be backed by something .”

So the argument is no longer against the idea of currency as a whole. Rather, it's an argument that a currency needs to be backed by something of value.

I would argue... Is there a difference?

By admitting a need for currency in the first place, you're acknowledging that everyone values assets differently. In other words, for a backed currency to mean anything, everyone would need to agree on a single “valuable” asset.

The discourse inevitably goes down the following path:

Me: “There is no single asset that everyone values, so a currency backed by an asset means nothing.”

Person: “That's not true. What about gold? If all electricity, technology, or government control was to disappear, you'd still have gold. Gold is gold. You can touch it. You can make jewelry with it.”

It's not that I don't understand the thought process. The problem is this…

Me: “I don't intrinsically value gold. I value real estate, food for my family, tech gadgets, etc. In fact, even my wedding ring isn't made from gold. You're telling me I would need to, on a monthly basis, hand over an amount of gold equal to a mortgage to buy a house? Even though gold holds no value to me?”

Person: “But people value gold. You can trade it for anything you do  value and it will be accepted… because it's gold.”

In other words, it's not about the intrinsic value of the metal. It's about knowing that people will accept it for what you do  value.

In trying to make the case for “inherent value”, we've just described a value through abstraction - or rather, a currency.

The US Dollar

I hate to break it to you, but the US dollar isn’t backed by any intrinsically valuable asset either.

“Federal Reserve notes are not redeemable in gold, silver or any other commodity, and receive no backing by anything This has been the case since 1933. The notes have no value for themselves, but for what they will buy. In another sense, because they are legal tender, Federal Reserve notes are "backed" by all the goods and services in the economy.” - United States Treasury

[ https://www.treasury.gov/resource-center/faqs/Currency/Pages/legal-tender.aspx ]

In other words, the US dollar is backed by nothing more than the trust that others in the economy will accept it for goods and services.

We’re already trusting an abstraction. Cryptocurrencies just happen to offer a more efficient abstraction that cannot be tampered with.

Cryptocurrency is a Ponzi Scheme

As an investor residing in the US, there’s one definition of a ponzi scheme that I care about. The SEC defines a ponzi scheme as follows:

“A Ponzi scheme is an investment fraud that involves the payment of purported returns to existing investors from funds contributed by new investors. Ponzi scheme organizers often solicit new investors by promising to invest funds in opportunities claimed to generate high returns with little or no risk.” -United States Securities and Exchange Commision”

[ https://www.sec.gov/fast-answers/answersponzihtm.html#PonziWhatIs ]

Ponzi schemes are no more prevalent in the cryptocurrency community than any other business community.

The vast majority of cryptocurrency projects go nowhere near “promising to invest funds in opportunities claimed to generate high returns with little or no risk .” In fact, leaders of the most successful cryptocurrency projects have routinely warned that investing in cryptocurrency is highly volatile and carries extremely high risk.

In other words, unless the cryptocurrency community is attracting new investors by claiming there is “little or no risk”, it cannot possibly be considered a ponzi scheme.

To insist cryptocurrency is a ponzi scheme is to dole out the “ponzi” label to any   system  with an aspect of marketing and investment opportunity.

To use that broad of a stroke, you’d be forced to accept the following examples as deserving of the “ponzi scheme” label :

  • Investing in Stocks and ETFs
  • Owning equity in any company - including private equity of a company you happen to work for
  • Starting a company and accepting any type of capital investment
  • Buying event tickets during the initial sale and selling at a higher price on reseller websites
  • Investing in the US dollar (we’ll return to this)
  • Investing in Real estate

To be fair, real estate actually was a ponzi scheme leading up to 2008 ,   as p eople bought houses they couldn’t afford at “little or no risk” - a false comfort provided by central banks and mortgage brokers. In theory, you could always sell your house to the next buyer at a better price. So the conventional wisdom suggested. Homes always appreciated in value.

Until they didn’t.

Few media outlets label actual ponzi schemes as such, and yet, articles continuously apply the label to the entire  cryptocurrency space. The “ponzi scheme” label is nothing more than a mechanism for banks and institutional money to play scare tactics against their competition.

What is the Potential of Cryptocurrency?

Lack of Inflation

Did you know:

  • When currency inflates, anyone holding that currency is being stolen from?
  • On average, the US dollar inflates ~3% per year?
  • Inflation compounds year over year?

Why Inflation is an Issue

Consider a family that has properly budgeted enough money to buy a full cart of groceries each week. After five years in the current inflationary system, the family could only fill their cart with 88% of what they once could. After ten years, the family could only take home 75% of what they once could. After 20 years, they’d be taking home just a little more than half.

This is the current system some accept.

In the case of the United States, a central authority (i.e. the government) can inject money into the economy out of thin air through quantitative easing , or simply printing money. In either case, the end result is this:

  • Value is “stolen” from those who had saved money.
  • Those taking on debt are rewarded.

Injecting money into the economy means you’ve simply created demand for actual value (labor and services) without adding any supply of such actual value (labor and services). Through classic supply and demand, prices of labor and services - and by extension, goods - rise when the US dollar is inflated.

Those holding US dollars can no longer be what they used to be able to buy, value has essentially been stolen from them. Families buying groceries did not make the decision to bailout AIG or Goldman Sachs after the 2008 financial crisis. And yet, they paid for it as the dollar inflated.

There are those in favor of centrally-controlled currency and “controlled” inflation - a rabbit hole that leads into the political realm. However:

One cannot argue for inflation without accepting the underlying premise that, “It’s OK for a central authority to change the rules halfway through the game, if it’s ‘for your own benefit’.”

History is littered with examples of central governments changing the rules to financial systems “halfway through the game.” Take a guess at h ow many of those instances ended in the benefit of the general public’s hard-earned money.

Cryptocurrencies Don’t Inflate

Enter cryptocurrency. For most cryptocurrencies (true cryptocurrencies), the maximum amount of coins is algorithmically set and cannot be changed.

The only way to change or inflate the currency would be to change the algorithm in the source code. Changing source code to inflate the coin would require a hard fork, which means a new coin would be created. In such a scenario, the original currency would still exist - unaltered and without inflation.

For example, Litecoin was one of the earliest hard forks of Bitcoin. Despite source code changes made to increase the maximum number of coins (4x), Bitcoin still exists uninflated. As does the community willing to support it.

In the future world of cryptocurrency, you simply never lose ~3% in earning power compounded year after year. Viewed another way, if you’re the type of person that puts money into a low-cost ETF, you’d essentially earn an extra 3% per year with zero risk.  

Decentralization

Decentralization is the most important aspect of cryptocurrency. If it weren’t for decentralization, cryptocurrency wouldn’t differ from other digital payment methods like, say, PayPal.

If you had to choose just one argument for cryptocurrency, it’s this:

Currency is built entirely on trust. If you trust fiat currency (like the US dollar) you are trusting government, the human ability to refrain from self-interest, and human error. If you trust cryptocurrencies, you are trusting math and computer science.

I don’t know about you, but in the context of honest and transparent processing of money and payments, I’m taking math and computer science over fallible humans.

So why is the technology behind cryptocurrency such a revelation? Why hasn’t it been available until now?

The answer is that it’s quite complex to create a system that allows the sharing of extremely sensitive information in a trustless manner.

The Byzantine Generals Problem

The Byzantine Generals Problem is an agreement problem pondered by academics of various fields since the early 1980s. It’s the problem that has been solved by blockchain - allowing cryptocurrencies to replace human intermediaries.

The Byzantine Generals Problem describes a situation where a group of generals are approaching battle and must agree upon a battle plan. They can only communicate with one another through messengers, of which there’s no guarantee of successful delivery. Additionally, there’s the possibility of disloyal generals sending messages that attempt to compromise the mission, or promote their own self-interest.

A successful solution would allow the loyal generals to reach a consensus despite unstable and malicious messaging.

The Blockchain Solution

The Byzantine Generals Problem is the same problem a decentralized currency like Bitcoin (or any blockchain) sets out to solve.

With any digital currency, how are consumers to trust that their money is actually transferred from one location to another? How does the system prevent from failing when transactions are sent from an unreliable device or internet connection? How does the system deal with hackers sending fake transactions?

In a centralized system, a bank or central authority is (ideally) held responsible for all of the above risks. That means they’re spending a ton of time and resources to ultimately solve the Byzantine Generals Problem. As with any human solution, it’s inefficient and subject to appeals and audits - which opens the door for the even less efficient legal system.

Blockchain powerfully solves the problem using an algorithm. The algorithm is run by all of the computers on the network and virtually guarantees accurate and trustless consensus of sensitive information.

That is decentralization.

Benefits of decentralized currency include:

  • Diversification:  You do not need to worry about the stability of any one company or group of large companies.
  • Security: There is no one company, bank, or government for hackers to target.
  • The use of currency cannot not be enforced upon people.
  • Lack of Manipulation: The c urrency cannot be inflated, deflated, or otherwise manipulated.

Cost Savings

Maybe you don’t care to get into the details of who you’re actually trusting when you put money into a bank account. Or possibly, you don’t care to analyze inflation and how it may be lessening your spending power.

Even if that’s the case, you should still be aware that cryptocurrency has the potential to drastically lower the fees you’re currently paying banks. By eliminating the middleman, cryptocurrencies also eliminate the following common fees among most banks:

  • Checking Account Fee
  • Minimum Balance Fee
  • Overdraft Charges
  • Returned Deposit Fee
  • ATM Fee
  • Foreign Transaction Fee
  • Lost Card Fee
  • Inactivity Fee
  • Account Closing Fee

In the cryptocurrency world, you are never charged any of these fees.

Once you’ve experienced a banking system without the “middle man”, you’ll instantly see the current system through a different lense, and realize just how much you used to pay to hold and transfer your own money.

Banking for Everyone

Many people in various areas of the world do not have access to banking simply because they cannot afford to pay for it. Most banks simply don’t see it as in their best interest to open a large number of modest accounts. The overhead isn’t worth the limited opportunity provided by those small accounts.

But what if there was no overhead?

The ability to provide banking to everyone  is one of the most promising opportunities of cryptocurrency. Cryptocurrency networks do not care what a transaction is “worth”.

How can this be?

The only existing overhead in cryptocurrency is the ability for the network to record transactions to the single source of truth known as the ledger. The computing hash power required for the network to arrive at consensus is the exact same  whether the transaction itself is worth $100 or $100 million.
